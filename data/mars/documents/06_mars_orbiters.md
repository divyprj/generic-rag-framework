# Mars Orbiters — Eyes in the Sky

While landers and rovers capture the public imagination, Mars orbiters have been the indispensable backbone of exploration. They map the entire planet at high resolution, detect mineral signatures invisible from the surface, relay communications from surface assets, and monitor weather and atmospheric dynamics over entire Martian years. Five orbiters stand out for their transformative contributions.

## Mars Global Surveyor (1997–2006)

Mars Global Surveyor (MGS) launched on November 7, 1996, and entered Mars orbit on September 11, 1997. After a 16-month aerobraking campaign that used atmospheric drag to lower and circularize its orbit, MGS began its primary mapping mission in March 1999 from a near-polar, sun-synchronous orbit at approximately 378 kilometers altitude.

### Key Instruments and Discoveries

- **Mars Orbiter Laser Altimeter (MOLA):** MOLA fired laser pulses at the surface and measured return times to create the most detailed topographic map of any planet. It generated over 600 million elevation measurements with a vertical accuracy of approximately 1 meter. MOLA data revealed the dramatic hemispheric dichotomy between Mars's smooth northern lowlands and heavily cratered southern highlands, and precisely measured the heights of Olympus Mons (21.9 km) and the depth of Hellas Basin (over 7 km).
- **Thermal Emission Spectrometer (TES):** Mapped global mineral distribution and identified large deposits of hematite in Meridiani Planum—the discovery that drove the selection of Opportunity's landing site.
- **Magnetometer/Electron Reflectometer (MAG/ER):** Discovered remnant crustal magnetic fields concentrated in the ancient southern highlands, proving that Mars once generated a global magnetic field through a dynamo mechanism—a finding with profound implications for the planet's atmospheric loss history.
- **Mars Orbiter Camera (MOC):** Captured over 240,000 images at resolutions down to 1.5 meters per pixel, revealing fresh gullies and evidence of recent water flow, new impact craters, and seasonal changes in polar frost.

MGS operated for nearly a decade, far exceeding its two-year primary mission. Contact was lost on November 2, 2006, following a software command error that caused the solar panels to rotate to an incorrect position, depleting the batteries.

## Mars Odyssey (2001–Present)

Mars Odyssey launched on April 7, 2001, and entered Mars orbit on October 24, 2001. As of 2024, it remains operational, making it the longest-serving spacecraft at Mars with over 23 years of continuous operation.

### Key Instruments and Discoveries

- **Thermal Emission Imaging System (THEMIS):** A multi-spectral camera operating in visible and thermal infrared wavelengths, THEMIS has mapped the entire Martian surface and identified mineralogical features including chloride salt deposits, olivine exposures, and hydrated silicates.
- **Gamma Ray Spectrometer (GRS) and Neutron Spectrometer:** Detected hydrogen—a proxy for water—within the upper meter of Martian soil. GRS measurements mapped vast deposits of subsurface water ice at high latitudes, with hydrogen concentrations indicating ice content up to 50 percent by mass in some regions. These orbital detections were later confirmed by the Phoenix lander.
- **Mars Radiation Environment Experiment (MARIE):** Measured the radiation environment in Mars orbit until a solar particle event damaged the instrument in October 2003.

Odyssey also serves as a critical communications relay for surface missions, having relayed the majority of data from both Spirit and Opportunity as well as supporting Curiosity and InSight. Its orbit was adjusted in 2009 to a local solar time that enables it to capture thermal observations during the late afternoon—optimal for detecting thermal anomalies.

## Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter (MRO, 2006–Present)

MRO launched on August 12, 2005, and entered Mars orbit on March 10, 2006. After six months of aerobraking, it began its primary science mission in November 2006 from a near-circular orbit at approximately 250–316 kilometers altitude. MRO is the most data-productive Mars mission in history, having returned over 50 terabytes of data—more than all other Mars missions combined.

### Key Instruments and Discoveries

- **HiRISE (High Resolution Imaging Science Experiment):** The most powerful camera ever sent to another planet, HiRISE captures images at a resolution of approximately 25 centimeters per pixel from orbit—sufficient to resolve features the size of a desk. It has imaged active slope features (recurring slope lineae), landing sites at extraordinary detail, avalanches in progress, and the tracks of rovers on the surface. HiRISE has returned over 700,000 images.
- **CRISM (Compact Reconnaissance Imaging Spectrometer for Mars):** A visible-near-infrared imaging spectrometer that mapped the global distribution of minerals including phyllosilicates (clays), sulfates, carbonates, and iron oxides. CRISM data have identified over 1,000 mineral-bearing outcrops and were critical for selecting the landing sites of Curiosity and Perseverance.
- **SHARAD (Shallow Radar):** A subsurface sounding radar that penetrates up to 1 kilometer below the surface, revealing buried ice deposits, layering within the polar caps, and subsurface geological structures. SHARAD data, combined with MARSIS data from Mars Express, have estimated that the Martian polar caps contain enough water ice to cover the entire planet in a layer approximately 11 meters deep.
- **Context Camera (CTX):** Provides grayscale images at 6 meters per pixel, and has imaged over 99 percent of the Martian surface—the most complete moderate-resolution image dataset of any planet.
- **Mars Climate Sounder (MCS):** Profiles atmospheric temperature, dust, and water vapor from the surface to 80 kilometers altitude.

MRO also serves as the primary data relay for Curiosity and Perseverance, with its high-gain antenna capable of transmitting data to Earth at up to 6 megabits per second—the highest data rate from Mars.

## MAVEN (2014–Present)

The Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution (MAVEN) mission launched on November 18, 2013, and entered Mars orbit on September 21, 2014. MAVEN is NASA's first mission dedicated to understanding Mars's upper atmosphere and its interaction with the solar wind.

### Key Instruments and Discoveries

MAVEN carries eight science instruments organized into three packages: Particles and Fields, Remote Sensing, and Neutral Gas and Ion Mass Spectrometer (NGIMS). Key findings include:

- **Atmospheric Escape Rate:** MAVEN measured the current rate of atmospheric escape, finding that the solar wind strips away approximately 100 grams of atmospheric gas per second. During solar storms, this rate increases by a factor of 10–20.
- **Historical Atmosphere Loss:** By analyzing isotope ratios (particularly argon-36 to argon-38) and extrapolating escape rates, MAVEN scientists estimated that Mars has lost approximately 65 percent of its original atmospheric argon to space. This implies that the early Martian atmosphere was substantially thicker—likely 1–2 bars of surface pressure—sufficient to support liquid water.
- **Aurora:** MAVEN detected diffuse auroral emissions at ultraviolet wavelengths across the planet, driven by solar energetic particles interacting directly with the atmosphere in the absence of a global magnetic field.

Since 2019, MAVEN has also served as a communications relay for surface missions.

## Mars Express (ESA, 2003–Present)

Mars Express, the European Space Agency's first planetary mission, launched on June 2, 2003, and entered Mars orbit on December 25, 2003. It carries seven scientific instruments and remains operational as of 2024.

### Key Instruments and Discoveries

- **High Resolution Stereo Camera (HRSC):** Provides color stereoscopic images at up to 2 meters per pixel, enabling detailed topographic mapping and 3D terrain models.
- **MARSIS (Mars Advanced Radar for Subsurface and Ionosphere Sounding):** A low-frequency subsurface radar that penetrates up to 3.7 kilometers below the surface. In 2018, MARSIS data provided evidence for a body of liquid water—approximately 20 kilometers across—beneath the south polar ice cap at a depth of about 1.5 kilometers. This controversial finding, if confirmed, would represent the most significant evidence for present-day liquid water on Mars.
- **OMEGA (Observatoire pour la Minéralogie, l'Eau, les Glaces et l'Activité):** A visible-near-infrared spectrometer that produced the first global mineral maps of Mars, identifying phyllosilicates, sulfates, and ferric oxides.
- **Planetary Fourier Spectrometer (PFS):** Measured atmospheric composition and detected methane—though subsequent measurements by TGO have placed stricter upper limits on methane abundance.

Mars Express also carried the Beagle 2 lander, which descended to the surface on December 25, 2003, but failed to fully deploy its solar panels and could not communicate. HiRISE images later located Beagle 2 on the surface in January 2015, confirming it landed successfully but with partially opened panels.
