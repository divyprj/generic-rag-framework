# Perseverance Rover and Ingenuity Helicopter

NASA's Perseverance rover and its companion Ingenuity helicopter represent the latest chapter in Mars surface exploration, combining advanced astrobiology instruments with groundbreaking aerial capability. Perseverance is the first mission explicitly designed to search for signs of ancient microbial life and cache samples for future return to Earth.

## Perseverance Rover

### Mission Overview

Perseverance launched on July 30, 2020, aboard an Atlas V 541 rocket from Cape Canaveral Space Force Station. After a 203-day cruise covering approximately 472 million kilometers, it landed on February 18, 2021, at 20:55 UTC inside Jezero Crater at 18.4447°N, 77.4508°E. The mission cost, including development, launch vehicle, and operations through the primary mission, was approximately $2.7 billion.

### Jezero Crater Landing Site

Jezero Crater is a 45-kilometer-diameter impact crater on the western edge of Isidis Planitia. Orbital observations revealed a prominent river delta on its western rim where an ancient river once flowed into a crater lake, depositing sediments approximately 3.5 billion years ago. Delta deposits are among the best environments on Earth for preserving biosignatures, making Jezero a prime target for astrobiology. The crater also contains carbonate-bearing rocks along its inner rim, potentially recording ancient shoreline chemistry.

### Sky Crane Landing and Terrain-Relative Navigation

Perseverance used the same sky crane landing system proven by Curiosity, enhanced with a new Terrain-Relative Navigation (TRN) system. During descent, onboard cameras compared real-time surface images against orbital maps stored in memory, enabling the rover to autonomously divert away from hazardous terrain. TRN reduced the landing ellipse from Curiosity's 20×7 kilometers to just 7.7×6.6 kilometers, enabling a safe landing within the geologically complex Jezero Crater floor.

### Rover Specifications

Perseverance has a mass of 1,025 kilograms, measures 3.0 meters long, 2.7 meters wide, and 2.2 meters tall. Like Curiosity, it is powered by a Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator (MMRTG) producing approximately 110 watts of electrical power. Its six aluminum wheels are 52.5 centimeters in diameter, slightly larger and with a redesigned tread pattern compared to Curiosity's wheels to improve durability.

## Scientific Instruments

Perseverance carries seven primary science instruments:

- **SHERLOC (Scanning Habitable Environments with Raman & Luminescence for Organics & Chemicals):** An ultraviolet Raman spectrometer and fluorescence instrument on the robotic arm. SHERLOC can detect organic compounds and minerals at a fine spatial scale, mapping their distribution across rock surfaces.
- **WATSON (Wide Angle Topographic Sensor for Operations and eNgineering):** A color camera co-located with SHERLOC that provides close-up images of rock textures and grain structures, functioning as a science-grade hand lens.
- **Mastcam-Z:** A pair of zoomable, multispectral, stereoscopic cameras on the mast with focal lengths ranging from 26 to 110 millimeters. Mastcam-Z can resolve features as small as about 0.7 millimeters at 2 meters distance.
- **PIXL (Planetary Instrument for X-ray Lithochemistry):** An X-ray fluorescence spectrometer on the robotic arm that maps elemental chemistry at sub-millimeter scales, enabling detailed petrology of individual mineral grains.
- **RIMFAX (Radar Imager for Mars' Subsurface Experiment):** A ground-penetrating radar that probes geological structure to depths of more than 10 meters beneath the rover, revealing layering, buried craters, and ice deposits.
- **SuperCam:** An upgraded successor to Curiosity's ChemCam, combining LIBS (laser-induced breakdown spectroscopy), Raman spectroscopy, time-resolved fluorescence, visible and infrared spectroscopy, and a microphone. SuperCam can characterize rock chemistry, mineralogy, and organic content at distances up to 7 meters. Its microphone recorded the first audio from the Martian surface.
- **MOXIE (Mars Oxygen In-Situ Resource Utilization Experiment):** A technology demonstrator that produces molecular oxygen (O₂) from atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO₂) through solid oxide electrolysis. In its initial run on April 20, 2021, MOXIE produced 5.4 grams of oxygen in one hour—enough for approximately 10 minutes of breathing for an astronaut. Over 16 extraction runs through August 2023, MOXIE produced a cumulative 122 grams of oxygen at purities of 98 percent or higher, validating the technology for future human missions.

### Sample Caching System

Perseverance carries the most complex and capable robotic sample collection system ever sent to another planet. Its coring drill can extract cylindrical rock cores approximately 6 centimeters long and 1.3 centimeters in diameter, which are sealed inside titanium sample tubes. The rover carries 43 sample tubes. As of early 2024, Perseverance has filled and sealed over 20 sample tubes, with a selection of duplicate tubes deposited at the Three Forks sample depot on the crater floor as a backup for the Mars Sample Return campaign. Collected samples include igneous rocks from the crater floor, sedimentary rocks from the Jezero delta, and regolith samples.

## Ingenuity Helicopter

### Design and Specifications

Ingenuity is a 1.8-kilogram solar-powered rotorcraft that served as a technology demonstration for powered flight in Mars's thin atmosphere. Its twin counter-rotating coaxial rotors span 1.2 meters tip-to-tip and spin at approximately 2,400 revolutions per minute—about five times faster than a typical Earth helicopter—to generate lift in an atmosphere with only about 1 percent of Earth's sea-level density. Ingenuity stands 0.49 meters tall, carries a downward-looking navigation camera and a 13-megapixel color camera, and communicates with Perseverance via a 900 MHz radio link. Its six lithium-ion battery cells are recharged by a solar panel generating approximately 6 watts at Mars.

### First Powered Flight on Another Planet

On April 19, 2021, Ingenuity achieved the first powered, controlled flight on another planet. Flight 1 lasted 39.1 seconds, reaching an altitude of 3 meters above the surface. This achievement has been compared to the Wright brothers' first flight at Kitty Hawk in 1903, and a small swatch of fabric from the Wright Flyer was carried aboard Ingenuity as a tribute.

### Operational Record

Originally planned for up to five flights over 30 days, Ingenuity far exceeded its technology demonstration objectives. Over nearly three years of operations, it completed 72 flights, accumulating over 128 minutes of total flight time and covering more than 17 kilometers of horizontal distance. Flight altitudes reached up to 24 meters and speeds up to 10 meters per second. Ingenuity transitioned from a pure technology demonstration to an operational scout, identifying scientifically interesting targets and mapping safe driving routes for Perseverance.

### End of Mission

On January 18, 2024, during its 72nd flight, Ingenuity sustained damage to one or more rotor blades during landing, rendering it unable to fly. NASA officially concluded the Ingenuity mission on January 25, 2024. The helicopter's extraordinary success has led NASA to plan rotorcraft for future planetary missions, including the Dragonfly mission to Saturn's moon Titan.
