# Phoenix Lander and InSight Mission

The Phoenix lander and InSight missions exemplify a complementary approach to Mars exploration: while rovers traverse the surface studying geology and chemistry, these stationary platforms drilled down—literally and scientifically—into the subsurface and interior of Mars. Together, they confirmed water ice in the Martian arctic, discovered perchlorates in the soil, and provided the first detailed map of Mars's internal structure.

## Phoenix Lander (2008)

### Mission Overview

Phoenix launched on August 4, 2007, aboard a Delta II 7925 rocket from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station. It landed on May 25, 2008, at 68.2188°N, 234.2508°E in the Vastitas Borealis region of Mars's northern arctic plains—the most northerly landing on Mars at the time. The mission was part of NASA's Scout Program, with a total cost of approximately $420 million. Phoenix was the first successful landing in a Martian polar region, following the loss of the Mars Polar Lander in 1999.

### Landing System

Unlike the airbag-assisted landings of Pathfinder and MER, Phoenix used a traditional powered descent with twelve pulsed hydrazine thrusters for terminal velocity reduction, touching down at approximately 2.4 meters per second. This heritage was derived from the canceled Mars Surveyor 2001 lander, which itself was a redesigned Mars Polar Lander.

### Scientific Instruments

Phoenix carried a suite of instruments designed to study the Martian arctic soil and atmosphere:

- **Robotic Arm:** A 2.35-meter-long robotic arm capable of digging trenches up to 50 centimeters deep in the soil and delivering samples to onboard instruments.
- **Thermal and Evolved Gas Analyzer (TEGA):** Eight single-use ovens that heated soil samples to temperatures up to 1,000 °C while a mass spectrometer analyzed the released gases. TEGA confirmed the presence of water ice and detected calcium carbonate and perchlorate compounds.
- **Microscopy, Electrochemistry, and Conductivity Analyzer (MECA):** Included an optical microscope (resolving features down to about 4 micrometers), an atomic force microscope (AFM)—the first ever used on another planet—with resolution down to about 100 nanometers, four wet chemistry cells for measuring soil pH, and thermal and electrical conductivity probes. MECA measured the Martian soil pH at approximately 7.7 (mildly alkaline) and detected soluble perchlorate ions (ClO₄⁻) at concentrations of approximately 0.5 percent by weight.
- **Surface Stereo Imager (SSI):** A panoramic camera mounted on a mast providing high-resolution stereoscopic images.
- **Meteorological Station (MET):** Provided by the Canadian Space Agency, measuring temperature, pressure, wind, and cloud cover including LIDAR observations of atmospheric dust and ice particles at altitudes up to 20 kilometers.

### Key Discoveries

#### Confirmation of Water Ice

Within days of landing, Phoenix's robotic arm uncovered bright white material beneath a few centimeters of soil. Over the following sols, this material sublimated (transitioned directly from solid to gas), confirming it was water ice rather than salt. This was the first direct confirmation of subsurface water ice on Mars by a surface mission, corroborating orbital detections by Mars Odyssey's neutron spectrometer.

#### Perchlorate Discovery

MECA's wet chemistry laboratory detected perchlorate salts (primarily magnesium perchlorate) in the Martian soil—a discovery with profound implications. Perchlorates are powerful oxidizers that could explain the ambiguous results of the Viking biology experiments three decades earlier. They also have significant implications for human exploration, as perchlorates are toxic to the human thyroid. However, certain perchlorate-reducing bacteria on Earth thrive on these compounds, keeping open the question of whether Martian perchlorates could support microbial metabolism.

#### Snowfall

Phoenix's LIDAR instrument observed precipitation in the form of water ice crystals falling from clouds at an altitude of approximately 4 kilometers, though the ice appeared to sublimate before reaching the surface. This was the first observation of active precipitation on Mars.

### End of Mission

Phoenix operated for 157 sols—well beyond its primary 90-sol mission. As northern Mars entered autumn, declining sunlight reduced power generation. The last communication was received on November 2, 2008. Subsequent orbital images confirmed that the lander was encased in dry ice (carbon dioxide frost) during the winter, with structural damage visible when the ice retreated.

## InSight Mission (2018–2022)

### Mission Overview

InSight (Interior Exploration using Seismic Investigations, Geodesy and Heat Transport) launched on May 5, 2018, aboard an Atlas V 401 rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base—the first interplanetary launch from the U.S. West Coast. It landed on November 26, 2018, at 4.5024°N, 135.6234°E on Elysium Planitia, a flat equatorial plain selected for its smooth terrain, ample sunlight, and subsurface accessibility. The total mission cost was approximately $830 million.

### Scientific Instruments

- **SEIS (Seismic Experiment for Interior Structure):** Provided by CNES (France), SEIS was an ultra-sensitive broadband seismometer capable of detecting ground motions smaller than the diameter of a hydrogen atom. It was deployed on the surface by the lander's robotic arm and covered by a wind and thermal shield to minimize environmental noise.
- **HP³ (Heat Flow and Physical Properties Package):** Provided by DLR (Germany), HP³ was designed to hammer a probe—nicknamed "the mole"—up to 5 meters into the Martian subsurface to measure heat flow from the planet's interior. The mole used an internal hammering mechanism to burrow incrementally.
- **RISE (Rotation and Interior Structure Experiment):** Used the lander's X-band radio communication system to precisely measure Mars's rotation axis wobble (nutation), providing constraints on core size and physical state.
- **Auxiliary Sensors:** Including a magnetometer, pressure sensor, temperature sensors, and wind sensors (collectively part of the APSS—Auxiliary Payload Sensor Suite).

### Key Discoveries

#### Marsquakes and Seismic Activity

Over its operational lifetime, SEIS detected over 1,300 marsquakes, ranging from tiny local tremors to events exceeding magnitude 4. The largest marsquake, recorded on May 4, 2022, measured approximately magnitude 5—the most powerful seismic event detected on another planet. Most marsquakes originated in the Cerberus Fossae region, a system of fissures approximately 1,600 kilometers east of InSight, suggesting ongoing tectonic activity.

#### Interior Structure of Mars Revealed

SEIS data allowed scientists to map Mars's internal structure for the first time. Seismic wave analysis revealed:

- **Crust:** Thinner than expected, with a two- or three-layer structure. Average crustal thickness was estimated at 24–72 kilometers depending on the location.
- **Mantle:** Extending to a depth of approximately 1,560 kilometers, with a lithosphere (rigid outer layer) approximately 400–600 kilometers thick.
- **Core:** A liquid iron-alloy core with a radius of approximately 1,830 kilometers—larger than previously predicted by models. The core is less dense than pure iron, implying significant amounts of lighter elements such as sulfur, oxygen, carbon, and hydrogen. The large core size indicates that Mars's mantle lacks the high-pressure mineral bridgmanite (perovskite), unlike Earth's lower mantle.

#### HP³ Mole Challenges

The HP³ mole encountered unexpected soil conditions. The Martian regolith at the InSight site formed a hard duricrust that did not provide sufficient friction for the mole's hammering mechanism. Despite two years of troubleshooting—including using the robotic arm to press on the mole and backfilling the hole—the probe penetrated only approximately 40 centimeters instead of the planned 5 meters. The experiment was officially concluded on January 9, 2021, without achieving its heat flow measurement objective. However, the troubleshooting process yielded valuable data about Martian soil mechanical properties.

### End of Mission

Accumulating dust on InSight's solar panels progressively reduced power generation. By December 2022, the lander could no longer operate its instruments. NASA received InSight's final communication on December 21, 2022, on sol 1445. The mission was officially declared over on December 21, 2022. InSight's seismic dataset remains the most comprehensive geophysical record ever collected on another planet and will be studied for decades to come.
