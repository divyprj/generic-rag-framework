# Early Mars Exploration Missions (1960s–1990s)

The first three decades of Mars exploration were defined by fierce Cold War competition, daring engineering firsts, and a gradual accumulation of knowledge that transformed Mars from a mysterious point of light into a geologically complex world. Between 1960 and 1997, the Soviet Union, the United States, and later NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory launched more than twenty missions toward the Red Planet, though only a fraction achieved full success.

## Soviet Mars Program

The Soviet Union was the first nation to attempt Mars exploration. The earliest launches—designated Mars 1960A and Mars 1960B—failed during launch in October 1960. The Mars 1 probe, launched on November 1, 1962, successfully departed Earth but lost communications at a distance of 106 million kilometers on March 21, 1963, before its planned flyby.

### Mars 2 and Mars 3

Mars 2 launched on May 19, 1971, and arrived at Mars on November 27, 1971. Its lander became the first human-made object to reach the Martian surface, but it crashed due to a steep descent angle and returned no data. The orbiter, however, operated successfully and returned data about surface temperature, atmospheric composition, and the gravitational field.

Mars 3, launched on May 28, 1971, achieved a historic milestone on December 2, 1971, when its lander became the first spacecraft to perform a soft landing on Mars. It transmitted data for approximately 14.5 seconds before contact was lost—likely due to a severe dust storm engulfing the planet at the time. The partial image transmitted remains the first data ever sent from the Martian surface. The Mars 3 orbiter operated until August 1972, completing 20 orbits and returning data on surface topography, atmospheric water vapor, and the Martian magnetic field.

Subsequent Soviet missions—Mars 4, Mars 5, Mars 6, and Mars 7—launched in 1973. Mars 5 successfully entered orbit on February 12, 1974, and returned 60 images over nine days. Mars 6 transmitted atmospheric data during descent on March 12, 1974, but crashed on landing. Mars 4 and Mars 7 suffered navigation failures and missed their respective orbital insertion and landing targets.

## NASA Mariner Missions

### Mariner 4 — First Successful Mars Flyby

NASA's Mariner 4 launched on November 28, 1964, and performed the first successful flyby of Mars on July 14–15, 1965, passing within 9,846 kilometers of the surface. The spacecraft carried a television camera that captured 22 close-up photographs covering about 1 percent of the Martian surface. These images revealed a cratered, Moon-like landscape—shattering popular expectations of canals and lush terrain. Mariner 4 also carried a magnetometer, cosmic dust detector, solar plasma probe, trapped radiation detector, and an ionization chamber/Geiger counter. Its radio occultation experiment determined that Mars's atmospheric surface pressure was between 4.1 and 7.0 millibars—far lower than previously estimated—and detected no significant planetary magnetic field.

### Mariner 6 and Mariner 7

Mariner 6 (launched February 24, 1969) and Mariner 7 (launched March 27, 1969) performed flybys on July 31 and August 5, 1969, respectively. Together they returned 201 photographs and confirmed the thin carbon dioxide atmosphere. They also measured surface temperatures ranging from −73 °C near the equator to −125 °C at the south polar cap.

### Mariner 9 — First Orbital Mission

Mariner 9 launched on May 30, 1971, and entered Mars orbit on November 14, 1971, becoming the first spacecraft to orbit another planet. It arrived during an enormous global dust storm that obscured the surface for weeks. As the storm cleared, Mariner 9 revealed a world far more diverse than the cratered terrain seen by Mariner 4. Over 349 days of operation, it returned 7,329 images covering 85 percent of the Martian surface at resolutions from 1 to 3 kilometers per pixel. Mariner 9 discovered Olympus Mons (the tallest known volcano in the solar system at approximately 21.9 kilometers), Valles Marineris (a canyon system stretching over 4,000 kilometers), evidence of ancient river channels, and the detailed morphology of the polar caps. It also provided the first close-up images of Phobos and Deimos, Mars's two small moons. The mission concluded on October 27, 1972, when its attitude control gas was depleted.

## Viking Program (1976)

The Viking program represented NASA's most ambitious Mars endeavor of the 20th century. It consisted of two identical spacecraft, each comprising an orbiter and a lander.

### Viking 1

Viking 1 launched on August 20, 1975, and its lander touched down on Chryse Planitia on July 20, 1976—the seventh anniversary of the Apollo 11 Moon landing. The lander operated for 2,307 sols (Martian days), until November 13, 1982, when a faulty software update severed communication.

### Viking 2

Viking 2 launched on September 9, 1975, and its lander set down on Utopia Planitia on September 3, 1976. It operated for 1,281 sols, until April 11, 1980, when its batteries failed.

### Biology Experiments

Both Viking landers carried three biology experiments: the Gas Exchange Experiment (GEX), the Pyrolytic Release Experiment, and the Labeled Release Experiment (LR). The LR experiment, designed by Dr. Gilbert Levin, yielded initially positive signals for metabolic activity in Martian soil. However, the Gas Chromatograph–Mass Spectrometer (GCMS) detected no organic molecules at the parts-per-billion level, leading most scientists to conclude that the LR results were caused by inorganic chemical reactions—likely involving strong oxidants such as perchlorates—rather than biology. The debate over the LR results continues in the scientific community to this day. The Viking landers also carried instruments for seismology, meteorology, imaging, and soil analysis. Viking 1's seismometer failed to deploy properly, while Viking 2's seismometer detected possible marsquake activity and confirmed wind-induced vibrations.

## Mars Pathfinder and Sojourner Rover (1997)

Mars Pathfinder launched on December 4, 1996, and landed on Ares Vallis on July 4, 1997, using an innovative entry, descent, and landing system that included a heatshield, parachute, solid rockets, and airbags. The total mission cost was approximately $265 million—a fraction of the Viking budget—demonstrating NASA's "faster, better, cheaper" philosophy under the Discovery Program.

### Sojourner — First Mars Rover

The 10.6-kilogram Sojourner rover was the first wheeled vehicle to operate on another planet. Measuring 65 centimeters long, 48 centimeters wide, and 30 centimeters tall, Sojourner was solar-powered and capable of traveling up to 1 centimeter per second. It carried an Alpha Proton X-ray Spectrometer (APXS) to analyze the elemental composition of rocks and soil. Over its 83-sol operational life (well beyond the planned 7-sol mission), Sojourner traversed approximately 100 meters, analyzed 16 rocks, and returned 550 images. The Pathfinder lander itself returned 16,500 images and recorded wind, temperature, and pressure data. The mission's final communication was received on September 27, 1997. Key scientific findings included evidence of conglomerate rocks suggesting past fluvial activity and chemical analyses indicating rock compositions similar to terrestrial andesites.
