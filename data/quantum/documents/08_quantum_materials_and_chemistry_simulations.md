# Quantum Materials and Chemistry Simulations

## Simulating Quantum Chemistry and Materials

The simulation of chemical systems and advanced materials is widely considered to be the most promising commercial application of quantum computing. Classical computers struggle to simulate the behavior of molecules because the computational resources required to represent quantum states scale exponentially with the number of electrons and molecular orbitals. Approximations, such as Density Functional Theory (DFT), are effective for simple systems but fail to model strongly correlated electronic states where electron-electron interactions are dominant.

Quantum computers can natively represent electronic wavefunctions because their qubits can be put into superpositions and entangled states that mimic the natural quantum behavior of electrons. Algorithms like the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) and Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE) allow researchers to model molecular ground state energies and transition states with high precision. By mapping molecular Hamiltonians onto qubit operators, quantum processors can simulate chemical reaction pathways, enabling the design of new molecules and materials without the need for slow, expensive laboratory synthesis.

## The FeMoco Catalyst and Fertilizer Production

One of the most famous target applications for quantum chemistry simulation is the nitrogenase enzyme, which catalyzes the conversion of atmospheric nitrogen gas ($N_2$) into ammonia ($NH_3$). Industrially, this conversion is achieved via the Haber-Bosch process, which requires high temperatures ($400^\circ\text{C}$ to $500^\circ\text{C}$) and extreme pressures ($15 \text{ to } 25 \text{ MPa}$). The Haber-Bosch process consumes approximately 1% to 2% of the world’s annual energy supply and accounts for nearly 1.4% of global carbon dioxide emissions.

In contrast, soil bacteria perform nitrogen fixation at ambient temperature and pressure using nitrogenase. The active site of this enzyme is a complex metal cluster called the iron-sulfur-molybdenum cofactor, commonly known as FeMoco ($[MoFe_7S_9C]$). Simulating the strongly correlated electronic structure of the active site of FeMoco is impossible on classical supercomputers. Research indicates that a fault-tolerant quantum computer running QPE could simulate the ground state of the FeMoco active space using approximately 111 logical qubits and a circuit depth of $10^9$ gates. Resolving the catalytic mechanism of FeMoco could enable the synthesis of biomimetic catalysts that replicate nitrogenase behavior on an industrial scale, reducing global energy consumption and carbon emissions.

## Next-Generation Energy and Solid-State Batteries

Quantum materials simulation also holds the key to developing next-generation energy storage technologies, particularly solid-state batteries. Current lithium-ion batteries rely on liquid electrolytes, which pose safety risks due to flammability and limit energy density. Solid-state batteries replace these liquids with solid electrolytes (such as sulfides or oxides), offering higher energy densities, faster charging, and improved safety.

However, designing solid electrolytes requires understanding complex chemical processes at the interfaces between the solid electrodes and the solid electrolyte. These interfaces undergo degradation, dendrite formation, and charge-transfer resistance that are difficult to model classically. Quantum computers can simulate the chemical pathways and diffusion mechanisms of lithium ions across these solid-state interfaces. By modeling the electronic structure of candidate transition metal oxide cathode materials and sulfide-based solid electrolytes, quantum simulations can accelerate the discovery of chemically stable interfaces, paving the way for long-lasting, high-capacity energy storage.

## High-Temperature Superconductors

Superconductivity—the conduction of electricity with zero resistance—occurs in certain materials when cooled below a critical temperature. High-temperature superconductors (HTS), such as cuprates (copper-oxide compounds) and iron-based pnictides, can superconduct at temperatures above the boiling point of liquid nitrogen ($77 \text{ Kelvin}$), which is significantly easier to achieve than the near-absolute-zero temperatures required by conventional superconductors.

Despite decades of research, the physical mechanism driving high-temperature superconductivity remains unresolved because these materials exhibit strong electron correlation. Quantum computers can be used to simulate the Fermi-Hubbard model, which is believed to capture the essential physics of HTS materials. By simulating the phase diagram of the Fermi-Hubbard model on a quantum processor, physicists can study how charge carriers pair up to form Cooper pairs in strongly correlated lattices. This understanding could guide the design of a room-temperature superconductor, which would revolutionize global power grids, electric motors, and magnetic levitation transport by eliminating electrical resistance losses.

## Corporate R&D Initiatives

Recognizing these disruptive potentials, global corporations have launched research initiatives to build quantum readiness. In the pharmaceutical sector, Roche has partnered with quantum hardware and software providers to simulate metabolic pathways and study binding affinities for drug targets. Biogen has launched collaborations to apply quantum algorithms to neurodegenerative diseases, including Alzheimer's disease, by modeling how candidate molecules interact with target proteins. Merck KGaA is exploring quantum simulations for both drug discovery and materials science, focusing on the design of advanced liquid crystals and organic light-emitting diode (OLED) materials. These corporate R&D initiatives ensure that these industries are prepared to deploy quantum chemistry solutions as soon as hardware achieves quantum advantage.
